Course Description
This course is an introduction to computer programming.

Its primary objective is to teach problem solving using the C++ programming language.

Emphasis will be placed on structured procedural programming with an introduction to object-oriented programming.

This course is designed primarily for computer science and related transfer majors.

Note: This course is a pre-requisite for many programming courses at Ohlone.
Course Objectives
Upon successful completion of the course, students will acquire the capability to:
  1. Employ the basic syntax and semantics to write programs.
  2. Manage program flow by using conditional and iterative structures.
  3. Construct programs modularly from subroutines/functions.
  4. Construct programs with 'arrays', pointers, and other structures.
  5. Demonstrate basic stream and file Input/Output.
  6. Apply software development methodology to programming projects.

Work Requirement
Keeping up with the work requirement on a weekly basis is essential in order to complete the course successfully.

Reserve 9 hours per week for this class based on at least three hours, which normally would have been spend in the classroom and 6 additional hours for hands-on exercises and studying, following the regular convention of 3 Units x 2 hours/week = 6 hours per week.

The time normally spent in the classroom will now consist of discussion board activity (see below) and following written directions of assignments.
Lab
The lab (hands-on) part of this course (listed in the schedule as +3.4 hrs/week or one unit of the four) is to apply the concepts from the lectures and readings into running code - with instructor assistance. In the past, this activity was done in a closed lab setting under direct supervision; however, with the advantages of the internet, we can use the idea of a virtual lab where each student can work self-paced wherever they like but still with assistance.

This assistance will take the form of a discussion board (more on this below). Raise your hand (virtually) for questions by describing clearly and posting the nature of the problem in a message. Within a day, either the instructor of fellow students will have responded and will continue the conversations until the matter is resolved.

Every student will be expected to participate in the discussion board, by one, asking questions or make comments from the hands-on assignments (if necessary) and two, help fellow students solve issues (we learn by helping) and contribute constructively.

Virtual labs (discussion board) will be accounted for based on weekly system reports on messages created, read, replied to, time spent, etc.
Discussion Board
Students will be expected to log on to a discussion board (db) several times a week and read all messages.

One point per week will be awarded for keeping up (reading) with the majority (80%) of the messages on the discussion board.

The db will be our main point of general classroom communication and lab discussion. The db is the place where all questions and comments will occur. Think of the db as a classroom where folks raise their hands and share concern with everybody else. It is highly recommended for students to jump in and help whenever necessary.

When a class participates fully with a discussion board, most questions can be answered within an hour or two, anytime of the week.

Use email only for private matters, such as grade or absences.
Quizzes
There will be weekly quizzes reviewing material from the previous week. Quizzes are taken online during a period of time, usually one week, as announced on the discussion board.

No quiz make-up available.
Academic Intregrity
Academic honesty is required for all work in this class.

Academic dishonesty occurs when students attempt to show possession for a level of knowledge or skill which they do not possess.

In this class, this translates as follows:

  1. It is considered *cheating* to take online quizzes and tests with others and/or share questions and answers.

  2. It is also considered *cheating* to copy code from someone else and making slight modifications. All code in this class must be typed by the student *from scratch*.

At any rate, most of the material in this class has been around campus for many semesters and can easily be modified and duplicated, however, in order to learn 'programming', there is no substitution for doing our own work.

Don't cheat yourselves :)
Final
The final consists of two equal parts:
  • an online comprehensive quiz
  • and a programming project announced at the end of the semester.
Grading Policy
The following percentages approximate the proportion of graded activities:
    Discussion Board: 10%
    Assignments: 50%
    Quizzes: 20%
    Final:20%

The course is graded as follows:
A=90-100, B=80-89, C=70-79, D=50-69, Credit=50+

Late assignments will be accepted up to the time they are graded (about a week or two) but will lose 10% of their value.

Student progress will be posted online.
